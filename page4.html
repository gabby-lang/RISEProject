The different labs at Ragon working actively on HIV

Allen's Lab

T Cell Immunotherapy, HIV Evolution and Transmission

The Allen Lab is interested in studying how the body's immune cells respond to HIV. They are looking for new ways to modify these immune cells called T cells Specialized white blood cells that identify and eliminate infected or abnormal cells in the body to maintain immune system effectiveness to better target and eliminate the HIV virus. The lab is also using cutting-edge gene-editing techniques Gene editing is a method to make changes to the instructions inside living things that control how they grow and function. to prevent the transmission of HIV and stop the infection from spreading. The main focus of the Allen Lab is to find a functional cure for HIV using recent advances in CRISPR gene editing CRISPR gene editing is a tool that lets scientists edit genes within an organism's DNA, like a biological cut-and-paste system. and CAR T cell immunotherapies CAR T cell immunotherapy is a treatment that boosts the immune system by modifying a patient's own immune cells to better target and destroy cancer cells. . They use a unique mouse model that has human stem cells, allowing them to study how HIV infects the body and how the immune system responds to it. Additionally, they use this model to understand the factors that influence how HIV is transmitted in the body.

Batista's Lab

B cells, antibodies, preclinical vaccinology

The Batista lab studies how our body's defense system works to create better vaccines and treatments for diseases like HIV, malaria, influenza, and SARS-CoV-2. They focus on a specific part of the immune system called B cells, which produce antibodies to fight infections. In their research, the lab looks at how and when B cells B cells are a type of white blood cell that play a key role in the immune system by producing antibodies to help fight off infections. are activated, aiming to improve the design of vaccines. They have studied how B cell receptors function and interact with other molecules. More recently, they have investigated how B cell metabolism plays a role in our immune response. Additionally, the lab works on creating new mouse models to test potential vaccines and strategies before trying them in people. They check how well these models recruit B cells, trigger changes in B cell receptors, and produce strong antibodies to fight disease.

Juelg's Lab

Natural infections informing immunotherapies

The Juelg lab focuses on connecting lab research with real-world applications to address infections effectively. They explore two main approaches: passive immunization Passive immunization is when a person receives ready-made antibodies from another source, temporarily boosting their immune defense against specific diseases. using special antibodies for immediate protection and active immunization through the development of novel vaccines. Passive immunization involves administering broadly neutralizing antibodies to individuals at risk of infection or those already infected. active immunization Active immunization is when a person's immune system is stimulated to create its own protection against diseases by introducing a weakened or inactive form of the pathogen or a part of it. aims to stimulate the body's immune system with new vaccine candidates to provide long-term protection against specific pathogens. Their work has the potential to significantly improve public health by reducing infectious disease burdens and responding to emerging threats efficiently. As they progress in linking laboratory research with clinical applications, the lab's efforts may lead to breakthroughs that positively impact global health and save countless lives.

Lingwood's Lab

Programing vaccine antibody responses

The Lingwood lab studies viruses like influenza and HIV, which can be difficult to target with vaccines because they trigger weak antibody responses. The lab's main goal is to understand how the immune system decides which parts of the virus to attack with antibodies Antibodies are proteins produced by the immune system that help the body recognize and fight off harmful invaders like bacteria and viruses. and then use this knowledge to create better vaccines. They focus on a type of immune cell called B cells and how they recognize specific parts of the virus. The lab has discovered special patterns in the genes of these B cells that help them recognize viruses. They use specially designed mice to study how these B cells can be redirected to target the most vulnerable parts of the viruses. Recently, they also found a system in the body that controls the level of a protein called interleukin-6 (IL-6) Interleukin-6 (IL-6) is a protein that plays a role in regulating the immune system and promoting inflammation in the body. , which affects the immune response. By manipulating this system in their experiments, they have shown that it can be used to boost anti-viral immunity Anti-viral immunity is the body's defense mechanism that protects against viral infections by recognizing and eliminating viruses. at the respiratory surface. All this research is aimed at improving our ability to create vaccines that can effectively protect against viruses like influenza, HIV, and bacteria.

Shalek's Lab

Single-Cell Genomics, Systems Immunology

The Shalek Lab focuses on studying and engineering human immune responses in tissues to find ways to prevent and cure autoimmune Autoimmune refers to a condition where the immune system mistakenly attacks and damages the body's own healthy cells and tissues. , infectious, and cancerous diseases. They use advanced experimental and computational methods to examine cells and tissues at a single-cell level to understand how they work and what goes wrong in diseases. By collaborating with researchers worldwide, they aim to identify solutions to maintain or restore human health. The lab develops new technologies that allow them and others to study cells and their interactions in a wide range of diseases. They share these tools with the global research community to advance scientific understanding. They investigate how immune cells in tissues communicate and respond to changes in their environment. They also study how cells interact with pathogen A pathogen is a microorganism, such as a virus, bacterium, or fungus, that can cause disease in a host organism. during infections and how tumors A tumor is an abnormal mass or lump of cells that can develop in various parts of the body, which may be cancerous (malignant) or non-cancerous (benign). avoid the body's immune responses. From their findings, they aim to develop therapies and preventive measures to improve human health. Their comprehensive approach not only provides valuable tools for scientific research but also transforms how we study and influence human immune responses in tissues. The ultimate goal is to find effective ways to tackle various diseases and promote better health outcomes for everyone.

Balazs’s Lab

Engineering Immunity Against Infectious Disease

The Balazs lab is focused on developing new technologies to engineer immunity and protect against infections. They study how the body interacts with pathogens like HIV-1 HIV-1 is a virus that attacks the immune system, leading to acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) if left untreated. and SARS-CoV-2 SARS-CoV-2 is a virus responsible for causing the COVID-19 pandemic, affecting the respiratory system and spreading between humans. , especially how the immune system responds to these infections. They are interested in understanding how pathogens can evade the immune system's defenses. To achieve their goals, the lab uses advanced methods such as gene transfer Gene transfer is the process of moving genetic material from one organism to another, either naturally or through deliberate methods, to introduce new traits or characteristics to create specific antibodies, animal models that mimic human infections, and deep sequencing Deep sequencing is a method used to analyze DNA or RNA samples with high precision and sensitivity, allowing for the detection of rare genetic variations and detailed insights into genetic information. to analyze the evolution of pathogens over time. By combining their scientific and engineering expertise, they aim to find innovative ways to prevent and treat infections and improve our understanding of how the immune system works.

Gaiha’s Lab

T cells, Vaccines, & Immune Control

The Gaiha Lab's main goal is to create vaccines that use T cells to fight infectious diseases and cancer. They study how certain T cells in some people can control HIV and SARS-CoV-2 infections effectively. They use advanced technology to identify specific targets for these T cells in the viruses' proteins. Their approach involves several steps, like analyzing protein structures and identifying crucial regions that the T cells target. They aim to use this knowledge to design vaccines that activate T cells against these targets to fight the viruses. Their ultimate goal is to develop vaccines for HIV, SARS-CoV-2, and other infectious diseases and cancer treatments based on T cell responses.

Kwon’s Lab

Mucosal Immunology, Microbiome, HIV, Clinical Research, Emerging Infectious Diseases

The Kwon Lab focuses on understanding how HIV affects the gut and the female genital tract The female genital tract refers to the reproductive organs and structures in the female body that are involved in the process of reproduction, including the ovaries, fallopian tubes, uterus, and vagina. , and how this impacts the acquisition and progression of the disease. They are part of long-term studies involving patients, particularly the Females Rising Through Education, Support, and Health (FRESH) cohort. They collaborate with research partners in South Africa, like the University of KwaZulu-Natal HIV Pathogenesis Programme and the Center for the AIDS Programme of Research in South Africa. To study these areas, the Kwon Lab uses advanced technologies. They investigate the immune responses in specific tissues like the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) Gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) is a part of the immune system located in the intestines that helps protect the body from infections and supports digestion. and the female genital tract. They study how the virus behaves in different parts of the body during chronic HIV infection. Additionally, they explore how HIV affects the body's response to other infections like Tuberculosis Tuberculosis (TB) is a contagious bacterial infection, primarily affecting the lungs, caused by the bacterium Mycobacterium tuberculosis. and how the microbiome (bacteria living in our bodies) influences the immune responses during HIV infection. Their work contributes to a better understanding of HIV and could lead to new ways to treat and prevent the disease.

Ndung'u Lab

Immune mechanisms of HIV control

The Ndung'u Lab investigates how the human body's immune system interacts with HIV, the virus that causes AIDS. They specifically look at immune responses that can protect or control HIV infection. Their main goal is to use this knowledge to develop vaccines or cures for HIV. They also study how the diversity of HIV affects how it spreads and how the body's immune system tries to control it. Additionally, they explore how HIV infection impacts the body's ability to fight against other diseases like tuberculosis. Overall, their research aims to find better ways to combat HIV and its effects on the human body.

Walker’s Lab

Immune mechanisms of HIV control

The Walker Laboratory's main focus is to study how the human body responds to viral infections, with a particular emphasis on HIV, which causes AIDS. They want to understand why some people's bodies are successful in fighting off HIV, while most others are not. (meaning the virus is still active in their bodies) and also from a special group of people called " elite controllers Elite controllers are individuals with HIV who can naturally control the virus in their bodies to very low levels without the need for antiretroviral therapy, maintaining a strong immune response. By learning from these patients' immune responses, they hope to discover new ways to combat HIV and improve treatments for the virus.
